The Importance of Your Ideas

I think what discourages many people from programming is this idea of learning a language - say Python - for the sake of knowing a language. If that is your goal, you will probably give up or lose interest, because programming is one of those things where you don't see crazy results right away.

I don't think learning to code with the intention of building the next Facebook is a good idea either. You'll get discouraged pretty quickly with that mindset.

The best and most enjoyable way to code, for me, is to build something that you want to create because you think it's cool - not because you think other people will buy or even use it.

I love the idea of being able to think of some crazy project that's based on a few core principles and push it out of my mind into a working piece of software using code. It's just so amazing to me.

And at first it's definitely hard to get those ideas out of your head or to even find them in the first place. But once you get started, you'll learn to sense when inspiration for a project is there. You can feel it in the air around you. For me, it's usually coupled with some illegible notes on small pieces of paper, maybe some penciled diagrams and basic math on the side.

Even if your idea seems crazy, or impossible with your current knowledge or skillset, I promise, from my own experience, it is always worth a try. You always learn something along the way.

I remember my first project: a Twitter snow day bot for Trumbull schools.

I knew what it would have to do - scrape the Snow Day Calculator for the percentage data, and then tweet that value if it exceeds a certain likelihood threshold - but I didn't know how I'd do it yet. I thought that building this bot would be so cool that I didn't care about having to learn how to use webscraping tools and external APIs like twitter4j. Every time something along the way worked, it felt amazing because I was getting closer and closer to this finished working product.

I think the beauty of these little projects is their perfect balance of simplicity and usefulness. There's nothing crazy to set up here, you just have to learn a few basic concepts. But in practice, it ends up actually being kind of useful - instead of having to repeatedly check the Calculator every time there's snow in the forecast, people can just follow the bot and get notified only when there's a sizable likelihood of cancellation.
